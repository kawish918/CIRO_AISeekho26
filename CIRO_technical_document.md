# CIRO — Crisis Intelligence & Response Orchestrator
## Full Technical System Document

---

## 1. Problem Context

Cities face fragmented crisis response. When a flood hits G-10 or a heatwave peaks in F-8, signals exist across social media, weather APIs, IoT sensors, traffic maps, and emergency calls — but they arrive in isolation, at different speeds, with different credibility levels. A responder watching five dashboards is slow. A rule-based alert system cannot reason across conflicting data. And once resources are dispatched, no existing system automatically recovers them when a crisis turns out to be a false alarm.

CIRO solves this by building a fully agentic, LangGraph-orchestrated multi-signal intelligence pipeline that:
- Fuses 5 heterogeneous data streams simultaneously
- Classifies crises using LLM reasoning with structured, validated outputs
- Allocates constrained resources using a deterministic, mathematically locked priority matrix
- Simulates response impact with before/after state modeling
- Notifies 6 distinct stakeholder audiences with tailored messages
- Detects and retracts false positives through field report contradiction analysis
- Recovers resources automatically via a TTL-based lifecycle callback loop
- Does all of this event-driven — consuming zero compute when nothing is happening

## 2. Signal Architecture: 5 Sources, 5 Distinct Roles

CIRO ingests 5 signal streams simultaneously. Each source has a distinct epistemological role. Relying on any single source produces either false positives (noisy social media) or false negatives (authoritative sensors with no context). The intelligence only emerges from their intersection.

### 2.1 Social Media — Fast, Noisy, Velocity-Driven

Social media is the fastest signal in any crisis but also the noisiest. A single viral post can look exactly like a mass emergency. CIRO addresses this through **mention velocity scoring** — rather than treating each post as independent evidence, it measures how quickly the volume of posts from a specific location is accelerating. A slow trickle of posts is noise. 23 posts per minute from G-10 in a 3-minute window is a credible signal surge.

Beyond velocity, each social signal carries a credibility score (source reputation), a geolocation confidence (GPS-verified vs. approximate area tags), and a contradiction level — a measure of how much the social narrative diverges from what other sources are reporting at the same location. A flood report from social media with no corresponding rain data and no water-level sensor spike raises its contradiction level, reducing its weight in the final composite confidence score.

### 2.2 IoT Sensors — Ground Truth Without Context

Sensors are physical ground truth. The water level in the G-10 stormwater drain is 1.8 meters and rising — that is an objective measurement that cannot be faked or misinterpreted the way language can. They cannot be generated by a viral panic post.

However, sensors lack context. A temperature threshold spike does not tell you whether it reflects a broken HVAC system on a rooftop or a city-wide heatwave. A water level spike does not distinguish between monsoon flooding and a burst municipal water main. Sensors provide the **credibility anchor** that validates social signal noise. When both a sensor threshold breach and a social velocity surge occur at the same location simultaneously, the composite confidence rises sharply because two independent physical systems are confirming the same event.

### 2.3 Weather API — Authority and Predictive Context

Weather data provides institutional authority. A "Heavy Rain / Critical" alert from the meteorological service is not citizen-generated noise — it represents systematic measurement and professional interpretation. It validates whether the physical conditions for a reported crisis actually exist.

More importantly, weather provides **predictive context**. Extremely high humidity combined with temperatures above 45°C does not just confirm a current heatwave — it predicts that conditions will worsen. This temporal forward projection enables the Analyst Agent to classify a crisis as having a long expected duration, which directly affects how many resources are committed and for how long.

### 2.4 Traffic — Physical Impact Proof

Traffic data is the physical world's fingerprint of a crisis event. A genuine flood in G-10 will immediately produce severe road congestion on surrounding arteries as residents flee, emergency vehicles converge, and normal transit is disrupted. This congestion spike is observable, measurable, and independent of what anyone is posting on social media.

Traffic data serves a dual role. First, it corroborates the existence of a physical event — you cannot fake a city-wide congestion spike. Second, it directly feeds into the priority scoring system through travel time: when roads are jammed, ambulance travel time from PIMS Hospital increases, which changes the mathematical priority score that determines which crisis gets resources first.

### 2.5 Emergency Calls — Official Response Activation

Emergency call frequency is the signal that confirms official responders are independently validating the crisis. When trained emergency operators receive a rising volume of calls from a specific location, it means the event has crossed the threshold from rumor to confirmed public emergency.

The Fusion Agent uses call frequency as a secondary corroboration mechanism. Low call frequency paired with high social velocity suggests possible panic without confirmed emergency. High call frequency with low social velocity suggests a real event that has not yet gone viral — a missed detection that rule-based systems would ignore.

## 3. Fusion & Triage Agent — Cross-Source Intelligence

The Fusion Agent is the system's sensory cortex. It receives all 5 raw signal streams simultaneously and performs cross-source correlation, producing a structured anomaly report that downstream agents can reason about reliably.

### 3.1 The Corroboration Requirement

The most important design decision in the Fusion Agent is the minimum corroboration rule: **an anomaly is only escalated if at least 2 independent sources confirm it**. This single rule prevents the entire category of false alarms caused by a single viral social media post triggering an emergency response.

The agent assesses each potential anomaly against this threshold. A flood report with corroborating water-level sensors, rain alerts, and traffic congestion scores high in composite confidence. A flood report with no rain, normal sensor readings, and light traffic is flagged as potentially suspicious — the contradiction is surfaced in the agent trace, and the anomaly either gets a very low confidence score or is suppressed entirely.

### 3.2 Credibility Scoring

The Fusion Agent scores five credibility dimensions per anomaly:
- **Mention velocity** — how fast is the social signal accelerating?
- **Geolocation confidence** — are the posts GPS-pinned or loosely tagged?
- **Sensor threshold status** — has an objective physical measurement crossed its limit?
- **Source reputation** — are the signals from verified accounts or anonymous posts?
- **Contradiction level** — how much do the sources disagree with each other?

These inputs combine into a single **composite confidence score** from 0 to 100. This number travels through the entire pipeline. A 90% confidence flood gets resources allocated immediately. A 45% confidence event gets resources allocated cautiously, with higher uncertainty range displayed to responders.

## 4. Crisis Analyst Agent — LLM Classification Engine

The Crisis Analyst receives fused anomalies and converts them into fully structured crisis objects. Every field in the crisis record is determined by the LLM's reasoning — not hardcoded rules.

### 4.1 What the LLM Decides

The Analyst determines:

- **Crisis type** — is this a flood, heatwave, accident, infrastructure failure, power outage, protest, or disease cluster?
- **Severity** — Low, Medium, High, or Critical, based on the signal data and the nature of the event
- **Confidence score** — how certain is the classification given the available evidence?
- **Affected population** — how many people are likely impacted? The system provides sector population hints (G-10 houses approximately 45,000 residents) but the LLM makes the final estimate
- **Affected radius** — how wide is the impact zone in kilometers?
- **Expected duration** — how long will this crisis last? A flash flood might resolve in 4 hours. A heatwave is an environmental condition that can persist for 48 hours or longer. There is no artificial cap — the LLM's domain knowledge determines the estimate
- **Spread risk** — is this likely to remain contained or expand to neighboring sectors?
- **Likely evolution** — a free-text prediction of how the crisis will develop over the next hours
- **Reasoning** — the full chain of thought explaining why each classification was made

### 4.2 Uncertainty Quantification

Every crisis carries its own uncertainty range, derived from the confidence score. A crisis classified with 70% confidence has a ±30% uncertainty band. This is displayed on the Dashboard and communicated to responders as a hedge — a high-uncertainty crisis should receive fewer committed resources until field verification can confirm it.

## 5. Event-Driven Orchestration — Zero-Idle Architecture

### 5.1 Why Polling is an Anti-Pattern

A naive agentic system polls all 5 signal sources at a fixed interval — every 30 seconds, every minute — and runs the full LLM pipeline regardless of whether anything has changed. This creates several compounding problems.

At 7 or more LLM calls per cycle, running the full pipeline 24 hours a day at 30-second intervals produces tens of thousands of LLM calls per day, the vast majority of which process data where nothing is happening and produce no actionable output. This is direct cost waste, it exhausts API rate limits that should be reserved for real emergencies, and it creates a system that is busy by design rather than busy by necessity.

### 5.2 Spike Detection at the Ingestion Boundary

CIRO solves this by moving the detection logic to the moment data arrives — inside the signal endpoints themselves, before any agent is involved. The moment a social signal with high mention velocity is ingested, or a sensor with a Critical threshold breach is received, the endpoint immediately fires an event trigger. The pipeline wakes up, runs the full analysis, and returns to complete silence.

Between events, the system is genuinely idle. No LLM tokens are consumed. No API calls are made. No compute resources are used beyond the minimal cost of keeping the web server running. The system is on alert, not on patrol.

### 5.3 The 30-Second Cooldown

Once the pipeline is triggered, a 30-second cooldown activates. Any additional spikes during this window are noted but do not launch a second pipeline run. This prevents "alert flooding" — where a rapidly evolving situation generates dozens of near-simultaneous spikes that each attempt to launch their own pipeline, creating overlapping and conflicting analyses.

Field reports are the one exception to this cooldown rule. When a field engineer contradicts an active crisis, verification is urgent. The field report trigger bypasses the cooldown and routes directly to the verification-only path, not the full pipeline.

### 5.4 Routing by Trigger Source

Not all triggers are equal. The system distinguishes between two fundamentally different situations:

When a data spike triggers the pipeline — new crisis possibly detected — the system runs the full 7-agent pipeline: Fusion, Analyst, Commander, Execution, Notification, Verifier, and conditionally Rollback.

When a field report triggers the pipeline — an existing crisis is being challenged — the system skips all resource-intensive agents and routes directly to Verifier and Rollback. There is no need to re-fuse signals, re-classify crises, or re-allocate resources to a crisis that is about to be retracted. This targeted routing is both computationally efficient and logically correct.

## 6. Resource Commander — Advisory Board (Multi-Agent Cognitive Friction)

### 6.1 Why Single-LLM Allocation is Insufficient

A single LLM prompt deciding where to send ambulances, rescue teams, and police units is a black box. It can reason plausibly but inconsistently — the same inputs can produce different resource distributions across runs. It can produce priority rankings that feel right in narrative form but are mathematically indefensible. It cannot be audited or explained to an emergency operations review board.

In public safety, every resource allocation decision must be defensible. When an ambulance goes to G-10 instead of F-8, there must be a specific, verifiable reason — not "the AI decided."

### 6.2 Phase 1: The Deterministic Priority Matrix

Before any LLM is involved in resource allocation, CIRO computes a locked Priority Score for every active crisis using a fixed formula:

**Priority Score = Severity Multiplier × Affected Population ÷ Google Maps ETA (minutes)**

The severity multipliers reflect the asymmetric urgency of different crisis types:
- Critical events receive a multiplier of 20 — an imminent life-threatening collapse or drowning scenario has 4 times the urgency of a High event even serving fewer people
- High severity events receive a multiplier of 5
- Medium events receive 2, Low events receive 1

The Google Maps Distance Matrix API provides the travel time denominator with real road-condition awareness. If traffic congestion adds 8 minutes to the ambulance route, that is reflected in the score — not assumed away with a fixed estimate.

**A concrete example from the standard demo scenario:**
- G-10 Flood: Critical (×20) × 45,000 affected population ÷ 12 minutes ETA = Priority Score of 75,000
- F-8 Heatwave: High (×5) × 52,000 affected population ÷ 6 minutes ETA = Priority Score of 43,333

G-10 receives resources first. The decision is mathematically provable, reproducible, and independently verifiable by any reviewer looking at the numbers. This is not a guess — it is a calculation.

Critically, these scores are computed in deterministic Python code and **locked** before the LLM is ever called. After the LLM Advisory Board produces its final output, the system silently replaces the LLM's stated priority scores with the Python-computed values. The LLM cannot hallucinate or misreport these numbers.

### 6.3 Phase 2: The Advisory Board Debate

The locked Priority Scores are handed to a three-persona internal debate loop. This is the cognitive friction mechanism — rather than one perspective making the final allocation decision, two contrasting perspectives argue it out before a synthesizer produces the final structured output.

**The Field Operations Commander** represents pure tactical urgency. This persona sees only the crisis severity, the priority scores, and the clock. Its argument is always toward maximum immediate resource deployment to the highest-scoring crisis: send everything available, establish control, and worry about secondary crises after the primary one is contained.

**The Civil Logistics Director** represents macro-level resource management and systems thinking. This persona reads the Field Commander's proposal and critiques it through the lens of unintended consequences. Deploying all rescue teams to G-10 leaves every other Islamabad sector with zero emergency capacity for the next two hours. Sending water tankers to a flood zone wastes them — they are needed for heatwave hydration stations. Completely depleting ambulance stocks violates minimum-coverage requirements for non-crisis medical emergencies.

**The Master Synthesizer** reads both arguments in full and produces the final structured allocation. Its output must cite both the Field Commander and the Logistics Director — the trade-off reasoning field in every allocation decision is required to reference both perspectives. This creates an auditable decision trail: judges and reviewers can see exactly what arguments were weighed before each resource was committed.

The full debate transcript is preserved in the Agent Trace logs and is visible live in the mobile app's Agent Log tab. The AI's reasoning process is not a black box — it is displayed in real time.

## 7. Execution Agent — Impact Simulation

The Execution Agent simulates the real-world impact of each response action before it is committed. For every category of allocated resource, it generates a structured impact assessment covering:

- **Before state** — what the situation looks like before the action: "Severe congestion on G-10 Margalla Road, zero medical units on scene, hospital intake queue building"
- **Response action** — the specific action being simulated: "Deploy 3 ambulances from PIMS via alternate route"
- **Expected after state** — the projected situation following the action: "Medical triage established within 12 minutes, primary casualties stabilized"
- **Response time improvement** — quantified improvement estimate in minutes
- **Congestion impact** — whether the response creates, relieves, or redistributes traffic pressure
- **Resource cost** — the commitment in resource count and estimated duration
- **Possible side effects** — the unintended consequences that the responder should anticipate

The side effects field is the most important innovation in this agent. It forces the system to reason adversarially about its own decisions. Deploying all available rescue teams to G-10 simultaneously might leave I-8 Sector with no emergency capacity for the next 90 minutes. A public flood alert might trigger mass evacuation that creates secondary traffic accidents on Highway 1. Hospital notification that draws all available surgical teams to flood trauma reception might delay critical elective surgeries. These downstream consequences are surfaced explicitly rather than buried in optimistic deployment projections.

## 8. Notification Agent — 6 Audience-Tailored Messages

A generic broadcast announcement fails crisis communication because the information needs of each stakeholder group are fundamentally different:

- **The public** needs specific, actionable safety instructions: which roads to avoid, where to go, what to do right now
- **Emergency services** need tactical coordinates: exactly where to deploy, what equipment to bring, who is in command on scene
- **Hospitals** need patient surge preparation: how many casualties to expect, what injury types, how much time they have to prepare intake capacity
- **Utility companies** need infrastructure isolation procedures: which water mains, electrical substations, or gas lines are at risk and what to shut down
- **Transport authority** needs rerouting coordinates: which roads are blocked, what alternative routes are available, whether public transit should be suspended
- **Media and the command center** need a factual summary: confirmed facts only, no speculation, no unverified casualty counts

The Notification Agent generates all six of these messages simultaneously for each active crisis, using the full crisis context — type, severity, location, allocated resources, affected population, and likely evolution — to tailor each message to its specific audience.

## 9. Verifier Agent & Conditional Graph Routing

### 9.1 False Positive Detection

The Verifier Agent runs at the end of every pipeline cycle. Its purpose is to check whether any field report in the system contradicts an existing classified crisis.

In the standard demo scenario, a WASA field engineer submits a report from G-10 stating that no flooding is present — the apparent cause is a burst water main on Street 47 that has been identified and is being repaired, with water levels already receding. The Verifier receives this report alongside the active G-10 Flood crisis and reasons about three questions:

First, does the field report provide a plausible alternative explanation for the signals that triggered the crisis classification? A broken water main explains the water-level sensor spike without requiring a flood.

Second, is the source credible? A WASA field engineer physically present at the location is a highly authoritative source — more authoritative than the social media posts that originally triggered the pipeline.

Third, does the explanation directly contradict the crisis type, location, and severity in a way that cannot be reconciled? If yes, the crisis is classified as a false positive.

### 9.2 Conditional Graph Routing

The LangGraph pipeline does not follow a fixed linear sequence. At the end of the Verifier node, the graph makes a conditional routing decision: if false positives were detected, the graph dynamically routes to the Rollback Agent. If no false positives were found, the graph terminates normally.

This conditional edge is the key architectural feature that enables true adaptive recovery. The graph structure itself adapts at runtime based on what the Verifier found. No external orchestrator needs to intervene.

### 9.3 Rollback Agent — Full Recovery

When the Rollback Agent fires, it performs three actions:

First, it removes the false-positive crisis from the active crisis list, immediately clearing it from the mobile app Dashboard and Map.

Second, it recovers every resource that was allocated to the false-positive crisis, returning those ambulances, rescue teams, and police units to the available inventory pool. These resources are now available for other crises.

Third, it issues a public correction notification to all 6 stakeholder audiences — acknowledging the misclassification, explaining the actual situation (burst water main), and retracting any previous public alerts. Transparency in false alarm correction is a formal requirement of emergency management protocols.

## 10. Resource Lifecycle & TTL Callbacks

### 10.1 The Static Depletion Anti-Pattern

Most demo crisis management systems subtract from a resource inventory when allocating and leave it depleted. After the first dispatch cycle, the ambulance count reads zero. The system cannot respond to anything else. Resources are committed indefinitely, even after a crisis resolves.

This is not how emergency response actually works. Ambulances return from hospitals. Rescue teams complete extractions. Police units are reassigned. A realistic system must model this return cycle.

### 10.2 Time-To-Live Resource Allocation

Every resource allocation in CIRO carries a Time-To-Live — an expiry timestamp computed from the LLM-predicted crisis duration. A flash flood with an estimated 4-hour duration produces allocations that expire approximately 1 minute later in the accelerated demo (scaled for demonstration purposes). A 48-hour heatwave produces allocations that expire approximately 12 minutes later.

Each allocation tracks its creation time, its release time, and its current status — active or released. The mobile app displays a live countdown timer for every active allocation, ticking down in real time. When the timer reaches zero, the status changes to "✓ Released" and the resource count in the inventory increases.

### 10.3 The ResourceMonitor Callback Loop

A background monitoring task runs continuously, checking every 5 seconds whether any allocation's release time has passed. When an expired allocation is found, three things happen simultaneously:

First, the allocation status changes to "released" and the resource count is restored to the inventory pool.

Second, an Agent Trace entry is created — visible in the Agent Log with the ⏱️ ResourceMonitor badge — confirming what was released, when, and what the new inventory count is.

Third, and most importantly, the pipeline is re-triggered with the trigger source "resource freed." This is the callback loop: freed resources may now enable a response to a previously resource-constrained crisis. The system re-evaluates the situation automatically rather than waiting for a human operator to notice that capacity has been restored.

### 10.4 Crisis Deletion Cascade

When a crisis is retracted — either through the Rollback Agent confirming a false positive or through manual intervention — all associated resource allocations are immediately released without waiting for their TTL to expire. The countdown timers on the mobile app stop instantly, resources return to the pool in real time, and the system is ready for the next event.

## 11. Degraded Mode & Robustness

### 11.1 API Failure Handling

Any production system must handle the failure of its data sources gracefully. CIRO implements a silent fallback mechanism: when any signal API fails to respond, the system immediately falls back to the last successfully cached response from that source, and logs a degraded-mode warning in the agent traces.

The pipeline continues running with slightly stale data rather than crashing. A Fusion Agent working with 2-minute-old weather data and 4 active signal sources is far more useful than a crashed system with no output.

### 11.2 Demonstrable Outage Simulation

For demonstration and verification purposes, the system includes a triggerable outage mechanism. A single API call can simulate the failure of the weather and traffic data sources, causing them to return service unavailable responses. The system's fallback behavior — serving cached data with degraded-mode trace warnings — is then directly observable in the Agent Log. A second API call restores normal operation.

This addresses the stress-test scenario explicitly stated in the challenge: "An API fails mid-response and the system must use cached or alternate data."

### 11.3 Additional Robustness Measures

**Duplicate crisis prevention** — Before posting a new crisis to the active list, the system checks whether a crisis of the same type already exists at the same location. This prevents the same event from appearing twice if the pipeline is triggered multiple times in rapid succession.

**Missing location handling** — When the LLM extracts a location that does not exactly match a known GPS coordinate, a fuzzy matching algorithm resolves it against a dictionary of Islamabad sector names and known landmarks. If no match is found, the crisis defaults to a central Islamabad coordinate rather than failing entirely.

**LLM score protection** — Even after the Advisory Board produces its final structured output, the system overwrites any priority score values the LLM may have generated with the pre-computed Python values. This prevents hallucinated or inconsistent scores from entering the operational record.

**Inventory floor protection** — Resource deduction operations are bounded so that inventory values cannot go below zero. Even if the LLM attempts to allocate more resources than available, the inventory system enforces the physical constraint.

## 12. Mobile App — 3-Tab Live Dashboard

The React Native mobile app is the human interface for the entire pipeline. It continuously polls the backend and presents live operational state across three tabs.

**The Dashboard tab** displays active crisis cards, each showing the crisis title, type, severity color code, confidence percentage, priority score badge, affected population count, and a brief description of the likely evolution. Below the crisis cards, a resource inventory grid shows current counts for each resource type. Below that, individual allocation cards show which resources are committed to which crises, with live countdown timers ticking down to release. The system status banner at the top of the screen shows which agent is currently active and what it is doing.

**The Map tab** displays a full-screen Leaflet.js map with CartoDB Positron tiles — a clean, light-colored base map designed for overlaid data. Each active crisis appears as a color-coded circular marker at its GPS coordinates. The marker color corresponds to severity: green for Low, amber for Medium, orange for High, and red for Critical. Tapping a marker reveals the crisis details. When a crisis is retracted through Rollback, its marker disappears from the map in real time.

**The Agent Log tab** is the transparency layer. Every agent action in the pipeline generates a ReACT-style trace entry — Observation (what the agent saw), Reasoning (what it concluded), Decision (what it chose to do), Action (what it actually did), and Outcome (what happened as a result). These entries are color-coded by agent: Fusion in indigo, Analyst in amber, Advisory Board in green, Field Commander in brown, Logistics Director in purple, Execution in violet, Notification in pink, Verifier in red, Rollback in orange, and ResourceMonitor in cyan. Judges can read the full chain of reasoning from raw signal to deployed resources in real time.

## 15. Four Design Principles

**Principle 1: Cross-Source Intelligence Over Single-Source Certainty**

No signal source is trusted alone. Credibility emerges from corroboration. A social velocity spike plus a sensor threshold breach plus a weather alert at the same location equals high confidence. A social velocity spike alone gets filtered. This principle is enforced structurally through the minimum-corroboration rule in the Fusion Agent.

**Principle 2: Event-Driven Silence Over Polling Waste**

The pipeline is completely dormant until data crosses a threshold. Zero LLM calls, zero API tokens, zero rate-limit consumption between events. The system is on alert, not on patrol. This principle is enforced at the architectural level — there is no scheduler or polling loop running the pipeline on a timer.

**Principle 3: Deterministic Anchoring of LLM Decisions**

LLMs excel at reasoning, narrative generation, and contextual interpretation. They fail at arithmetic consistency and verifiable mathematical claims. CIRO uses LLMs for what they are good at — classification, debate, explanation — and uses deterministic Python for what they are bad at — priority scores, inventory math, routing logic. The boundary between these two domains is enforced through post-processing overwrite: whatever the LLM claims the priority score is, Python's computed value takes precedence.

**Principle 4: Adaptive Resource Lifecycles Over Static Dispatch**

Real emergencies do not end cleanly on a fixed schedule. Resources get committed, crises evolve, false positives emerge, conditions change. A system that only subtracts from its inventory and never recovers it cannot handle sustained incident response. CIRO's TTL lifecycle model, conditional rollback graph routing, and automated resource recovery callbacks simulate the adaptive feedback loop that real emergency management systems require — but which traditional rule-based dispatch systems are fundamentally incapable of implementing.
